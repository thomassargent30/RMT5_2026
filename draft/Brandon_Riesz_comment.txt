The Riesz representation theorem is very elegant. It has a flavor of almost a]
 more complicated version of something like the mean value theorem. However, something isn't clear
 to me in the Riesz representation argument in the Appendix A. I think there is a skipped step.

You show the uniqueness of an element of N_complement, where N is the nullspace of the linear function.]
 However, you haven't demonstrated that N_complement is indeed X\N_complement, and therefore that there exists 
 such an element if X!= N_complement.  I think intuitively this is clear, since by linearity of \phi, if \phi(y) !=0, 
 then clearly it contains at least some additive components that are orthogonal, but this is not immediately obvious. 
 You also technically haven't shown that the nullspace is additively closed, only topologically closed, but I
 think this is easy enough for the reader to follow from the property of inner product spaces <x+y,z>= <x,z>+<y,z>.

Secondly, something also doesn't make sense. You argue by the linear *independence* of x_1, x_2 we can find some
 a, b s.t. a\phi(x_1) -b\phi(x_2)=0. This holds true I think even if x_1, x_2 are linearly dependent, since \phi is a
 linear functional that goes to the reals, no? We can always find a trivial a,b s.t. b=\phi(x_1)/\phi(x_2), a= 1, that
 satisfies this. This does, however, imply the contradiction you mention.
