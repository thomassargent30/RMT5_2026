Here are my comments and suggestions for the updated version of pages 30 to 40.

- There is a repetition of what P^(k)_(i,j) stands for: the first explanation is at the bottom of page 30,
 and the second is after equation (2.2.9) on page 35.

- Definition 2.2.1: I find the following to be conceptually clearer for part i.: for any nonnegative integer T, 
if e_i in X_tilde is visited at time t=T, only states e_j in X_tilde are visited at time t >= T. I believe that the
 two definitions are equivalent for time-invariant Markov chains. The advantage is that it has the following interpretation:
 once the Markov chain reaches the absorbing subset, it cannot escape it. Alternatively, if you keep your current definition,
 you could mention that the two are equivalent and encourage the reader to prove it himself/herself.

- Example on page 36: you might want to consider explicitly specifying the state space as {[1, 0], [0, 1]} since n=2.

- Right eigenvector discussion on page 37: Unless the dot in "Py_bar." has a mathematical meaning, Py_bar is an n x 1 vector 
so the ith column would only have a sensible interpretation if i=1. You could either refer to the ith entry of the vector or 
alternatively, transpose Py_bar. Additionally, it might be a good idea to reorganize the discussion because you ask the reader 
to think about the ith column of Py_bar twice. I would start by supposing that y_bar is a right eigenvector, and then expand the 
ith entry of Py_bar.

- Definition 2.2.4: it might be worth reiterating that the state space consists of unit vectors because y_bar_i = y_bar_j 
for all i, j is true only if the state is represented using unit vectors. Additionally, it might be a good idea to describe the 
relationship between an invariant random variable and an ergodic distribution. Finally, since a distribution may or may not be
 ergodic depending on the random variable that it governs, I would personally prefer to refer to pi(y_bar) as being ergodic 
 instead of simply pi to make this dependence explicit. 

- Remarks on page 38 and 39: I think that the expression "extreme point of the convex set S" is a little imprecise because 
any point on the boundary of S could be interpreted as extreme. If my understanding is correct, I would state that the basis 
stationary distributions are ergodic stationary, then treat them vertices and state that the set of all stationary distributions
 is the convex hull of the set of vertices.

- Example 2 on page 39 and example 3 on page 40: the processes are ergodic when alpha_1 and alpha_2 equal to 0 regardless 
of the value of gamma. These cases must be excluded to preserve the validity of the argument.

- Some minor formatting comments: vector dimensions are sometimes parenthesized and other times not. They are parenthesized 
in line 4 of page 30, line 4 & 5 of page 31 and in the second line of the remark on page 38. However, they are not parenthesized 
in line 2 & 3 of page 30. Also, unconditional expectations are sometimes not bracketed like in line 3 of page 35, at the bottom of page 36 and in equation (2.2.13).
