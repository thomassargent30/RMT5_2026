We study how a decision maker who knows two possible probability distributions for successive i.i.d. wage draws that nature makes optimally uses conditional probabilities to infer which of the two distributions nature is drawing from.

In effect, the decision maker does not know a hidden "state" or "parameter" that indexes whether nature draws from FFF or from GGG.

He does know that that "state" or "parameter" can possibly take one of only two values, say 000 or 111.

Our decision maker uses an unknown parameter to index whether nature permanently draws from density fff or from density ggg as a random variable with a known (prior) probability π−1\pi_{-1}π−1​. The parameter takes one of two values that indexes whether nature is drawing from fff or ggg. He use a "prior" (meaning "before seeing any data") probabilty mass function to express his uncertainty about this parameter.

Our Bayesian statistician then pretends that he ``knows the truth'' from the beginning: by this we mean that before seeing any data, the Bayesian statistician knows a joint probability distribution over histories wtw^twt and a random event that tells how at time −1-1−1 nature once and for all permanently selected either density fff or density ggg.

With these things in place, the decision maker uses that joint distribution to compute the conditional distribution of the random variable indexing fff or ggg, conditional on an observed history wtw^twt.

Computing a conditional distribution in this way is often called ``Bayes law''.
